Hypothesis 3: Regional Career Length Differences

Research Question: How do career lengths differ across regions?

Hypothesis: Eastern regions (LCK, LPL) produce players with longer average careers than Western regions (LEC, LCS) due to more established infrastructure and larger player bases.

Summary

992 Players Analyzed
LCK Longest Avg Career (3.34 seasons)
LCS Shortest Major League (1.63 seasons)
1.71 LCK vs LCS Gap (seasons)

Key Finding

Analysis of 992 players across 10 leagues confirms significant regional differences in career length (Kruskal-Wallis H = 74.27, p < 0.0001). LCK players have the longest average careers at 3.34 seasons, followed by LEC (2.80) and LPL (2.55). LCS has the shortest major league average at just 1.63 seasons. The East vs. West gap is statistically significant (p = 0.020), partially supporting the hypothesis — however, LEC outperforms LPL, meaning the East/West divide is not as clean as predicted. LCK's advantage is significant against every other major league.

Data limitation

Coverage is 57% of all player-seasons. LCS is notably underrepresented (83 players vs. the expected ~200+), which may compress its career length estimate. Results for LCS should be interpreted with caution.

Implications

Support for Hypothesis

  • The East vs. West career length gap is statistically significant (p = 0.020), providing partial support for the hypothesis.
  • LCK has the longest career length of any major league (3.34 seasons) and is significantly longer than LPL, LEC, and LCS individually.
  • LCS has the shortest major league career at 1.63 seasons — more than 2 seasons shorter than LCK on average.
  • 60 of the 157 players with 5+ season careers come from LCK (38%), despite LCK not having the most players.
  • The overall Kruskal-Wallis test (H = 74.27, p < 0.0001) confirms that regional differences in career length are highly significant across all leagues.

Nuance: LEC Outperforms LPL

  • Contrary to the hypothesis, LEC (West, 2.80) has a longer average career than LPL (East, 2.55), and the difference is not statistically significant (p = 0.123).
  • This suggests the East/West framing oversimplifies — LCK specifically has unique structural advantages (established franchising, strong fan support, longer organizational history) rather than Eastern leagues as a whole.
  • LPL's large player pool (334 players — the largest in the dataset) may create higher turnover as more talent competes for spots, shortening average careers.

Next Steps & Limitations

  • LCS sample (83 players) is likely underrepresented due to missing Leaguepedia data — re-run after full fetch completes.
  • Control for confounding variables: debut era, role, and starting tier all correlate with career length.
  • Investigate whether career length trends within each region are changing over time (e.g., has LCS improved?)
  • Analyze import players — many LCK/LPL players are from the same country, which may inflate regional averages.
